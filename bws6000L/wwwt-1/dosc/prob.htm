Problem:
                    Operating systems were introduced many decades ago to facilitate computer applications to share resources, and provide abstract interfaces to hardware, in addition to other administrative functions. The OSs were small, efficient, and easier to use as they were supporting small set of applications. Since then, OSs have become large, complex, and constantly changing to support a variety of computer applications including: home, entertainment, and office. Since last 25 years, Microsoft has released over 20 major versions of OSs for PCs. Similarly, in last ten years, there were over a dozen releases of Linux operating system. Latest Microsoft Windows XP consists of 40 million lines of code, and latest Linux consists of 30 million lines of code. There are thousands of programming bugs when a new OS version is released. There are also many security flaws in current OSs, and they constantly find new holes right after they fix the existing ones. There is no end in sight for the monstrous growth and complexity of OSs in near future.

 

                As the OS gets large, complex, and change periodically, it has a ripple effect on the computer applications and their longevity and security.  It is a daunting problem, and there is no one to address it, as every one takes it for granted to cope with the existing OS and its related tools and environments. Due to a change in OS, often there is a need to change the whole computer system including: hardware, software, and tools, which cost tremendous money and resources to industry and also individual customers. This problem manifests into rapid obsolescence in information technology, people's skills, and resources.
